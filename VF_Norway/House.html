Norway's Traditional
Houses

For over a thousand years, Norwegians have built homes designed to withstand harsh winters and blend naturally into the landscape. The most iconic style is the turf-roofed laftehus — a log cabin whose sod rooftop sprouts with living grass, insulating the home against bitter cold and heavy snowfall.

Turf-roof cabin · Norwegian highlands
Rorbu · Lofoten islands
Log cabin · Viking-era craft

These grass-roofed houses, known as torvtak, date back to the Viking Age and remained common throughout rural Norway well into the 20th century. The thick log walls were crafted using a traditional notching technique called laft, interlocking timber corners without nails — a method so durable that many original structures still stand today.

Along the coast, the rorbu — a distinctive red fisherman's cabin perched on stilts above the water — became a symbol of Norwegian maritime life. Originally built to house seasonal fishermen in the Lofoten islands, these humble dwellings are now treasured cultural landmarks and popular heritage stays for visitors exploring Norway's fjord country.
